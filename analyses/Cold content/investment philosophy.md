# Investment Philosophy

## The Core Idea

The Hong Kong market misprices complexity. Family conglomerates, holding structures, and spinoffs trade at deep discounts to the sum of their parts — not because the assets are worthless, but because most investors apply a single multiple to a business that requires four separate valuations.

The opportunity is not in predicting macro. It is in doing the work no one else takes the time to do.

---

## The Method

**Step 1 — Identify the asset**

Find a company where the market is pricing the whole as less than the sum of its parts. The signal is usually in the filing: a self-disclosed NAV, a disclosed book value well above market cap, or a subsidiary that trades on-exchange at a higher valuation than the parent.

**Step 2 — Read the balance sheet**

Apply a SOTP (sum-of-parts) valuation. Each major asset line is valued separately against comparable listed peers, then aggregated. The gap between that total and the current market cap is the documented discount.

> **NAV (Net Asset Value)** = Total Assets at market value − Total Liabilities

NAV differs from book value: book value uses historical cost, NAV uses what each asset would fetch today. For a Central Hong Kong office building acquired in 1990, the gap between the two is enormous.

**Step 3 — Find the catalyst**

Without a catalyst, cheap is just a description. With one, it is a thesis.

A catalyst is a near-term, observable corporate action that forces the market to reprice the discount within a defined window:

- A share buyback at a price below NAV
- An asset disposal that returns cash to shareholders
- A privatisation offer from the controlling family
- A dividend increase that signals confidence in cash generation

**Step 4 — Size the position**

Position sizing follows from the asymmetry: upside (discount compression) vs. downside (discount widens, or governance trap materialises). A position where the upside is 3x the downside, in a market that has been written off for three years, does not require optimism. It requires arithmetic.

## What We Look For

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Discount to SOTP/NAV | > 30% |
| P/E ratio | < 8x |
| Near-term catalyst | Identified, datable |
| Mainland developer exposure | Disclosed and provisioned |
| Related-party transactions | Read and assessed |

---

## What We Avoid

**Cheap without a catalyst.** A company can trade at 50% of NAV for a decade if management has no incentive to close the gap. The discount must have a mechanism.

**The China label trap.** CK Hutchison earns 12% of revenues from Greater China. Applying a China discount to its Rotterdam, Panama, and Colombo ports is a category error. We read the revenue breakdown before applying any country risk premium.

**Governance traps.** Family-controlled conglomerates do not always act in minority shareholder interests. Related-party transactions, below-market asset transfers, and family-first dividend policies are recurring features of this market. The discount is sometimes correct pricing. Separating the two requires reading the related-party notes. That is not optional.

## Key Concepts

### NAV Discount

The gap between what the market pays for a company and what its assets are worth if valued individually.

> **Example — Great Eagle Holdings:** The company owns Langham Hotels, Central office buildings, and retail properties. Great Eagle's own investor presentation estimates a discount of 88% to NAV. The stock trades at twelve cents on the dollar of its own assessed asset value.

### SOTP (Sum-of-Parts)

A valuation method that values each business segment or asset separately, then adds them together. The total is compared to the current market cap to size the discount.

### Catalyst

The event that forces price discovery. Without it, the discount can persist indefinitely. The catalyst must be:
- Near-term (identifiable within 12–18 months)
- Observable (visible in a public filing or announcement)
- Economically meaningful (large enough to move the stock)

## The Market Context

Hong Kong is not a proxy for the Chinese economy. The legal system runs on English common law. The currency has been pegged to the US dollar since 1983. The exchange has operated continuously since 1891.

The institutional coverage on mid-cap HK conglomerates is thin. Analyst reports are rare. Bloomberg consensus estimates are often stale. Fewer eyes means slower price discovery — and slower price discovery means the discount can persist longer than it should.

That is the structural advantage. It does not require an edge on information. It requires an edge on attention.
